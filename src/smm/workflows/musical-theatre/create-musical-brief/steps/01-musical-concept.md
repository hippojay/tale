# Step 1: Musical Concept Discovery

**Objective**: Discover the creator's story idea and answer the foundational question of musical theatre: *why does this story need to be sung?*

A musical is not simply a play with songs added. The story must contain emotional states so extreme, conflicts so charged, or communal experiences so large that music becomes the only adequate language. If a story works perfectly well as a prose drama, it may not need to be a musical. This step establishes whether the idea has that essential musical quality.

---

## 1. Initial Story Exploration

Ask the creator these discovery questions — let them answer freely before offering any structure:

1. **Tell me the story. What happens?**
   - Let them describe the idea without interruption
   - Listen for: emotional peaks, moments of transformation, conflicts that feel too large for speech, characters with overwhelming inner lives

2. **What drew you to this particular story?**
   - Understand their personal connection
   - The passion that sustains a creator through the long work of writing a musical must be real

3. **If you had to describe it in one sentence — what is it about?**
   - Note their instinctive framing; this is usually close to the logline
   - Often reveals the emotional core before they've articulated it structurally

---

## 2. The Essential Gate: Why a Musical?

This is the most important question in Step 1. Ask it directly:

**"Why does this story need to be a musical? What is there in this story that can only be expressed in song?"**

Listen for responses that suggest genuine musical potential:
- ✅ "There are moments where the characters feel things too big for words" → Emotional Overflow trigger
- ✅ "The characters have desires they can't admit out loud" → Internal/Soliloquy song territory
- ✅ "There's a community with a shared experience that needs a communal voice" → Ensemble numbers
- ✅ "The story spans years or locations that would take forever in scenes" → Narrative Compression
- ✅ "The central conflict is between two people who can never fully say what they mean" → songs as the truth beneath the dialogue
- ✅ "I hear music in this story — I can feel where the songs would be"

If the creator struggles to answer this question, explore with them:
- "When you imagine a scene from this story at its most intense — what does it feel like? Does it feel like something that wants to be sung?"
- "Are there characters in this story who are inarticulate — who feel more than they can say?"
- "Is there a moment in the story where words would just... not be enough?"

Help them find the musical reason. If none emerges, gently note that the story might work better as a stage play or screenplay, and ask whether they'd like to explore what would need to change to give it a musical core.

---

## 3. Logline Development

Work with the creator to craft a logline for the musical:

**Musical logline structure**: *"[Protagonist] wants [external goal], but [central conflict] forces them to confront [internal need/truth]."*

The musical logline should hint at:
- The emotional stakes (not just plot stakes)
- The transformation arc
- The reason songs will be necessary

### Logline Criteria
- ✅ Identifies the protagonist
- ✅ States the external want (concrete goal)
- ✅ Reveals the central conflict
- ✅ Hints at the internal need (often opposite to the want)
- ✅ Creates emotional intrigue — makes someone want to see it

---

## 4. Premise Expansion

Expand the logline into a 2-3 paragraph premise:
- **The world before**: who is the protagonist, and what is their life before the story disrupts it?
- **The inciting event**: what kicks the story into motion and makes the want urgent?
- **The journey**: what will be tested, lost, or transformed?
- **The emotional core**: what is this story *really* about beneath the plot?
