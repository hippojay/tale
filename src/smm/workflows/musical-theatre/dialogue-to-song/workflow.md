# Dialogue to Song Workflow

## Objective
Identify the precise moment in a scene where speech must become song, classify the trigger and voice, and produce a complete Song Brief for the lyricist.

This is the core dramaturgy of musical theatre: the point where emotion outpaces language. Alice identifies it; Larry writes it.

## Prerequisites
- The scene or scene section where a song is suspected or planned
- Character context (who they are, what they want, what they know at this moment)
- Position in the musical structure (act, scene, structural function)

## Steps

### 1. Read the Scene
- Identify the scene's emotional arc: where does it start, where does it peak?
- Mark the line where the character's speech starts to strain — where the words feel insufficient
- Ask: *what is this character feeling that they cannot fully say?*

### 2. Apply the Song Trigger Framework
Determine which trigger(s) apply to this moment:

| # | Trigger | Signal in the scene |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Declaration of Want** | Character has been denied or frustrated; their deepest desire must be voiced aloud |
| 2 | **Emotional Overflow** | Feeling (love, grief, joy, fear) has built to the point speech cannot contain it |
| 3 | **Conflict Escalation** | Argument between two or more characters has reached a level speech cannot escalate further |
| 4 | **Decision/Revelation** | Character has just understood something that changes everything; must process it internally |
| 5 | **Communal Expression** | Group of characters must feel or act as one; individual speech fragments |
| 6 | **Comic Exaggeration** | Situation has become so absurd only musical exaggeration can match it |
| 7 | **Seduction/Persuasion** | Character is actively winning over another person — or the audience — through sustained charm or argument |
| 8 | **Narrative Compression** | Story that would take too many scenes to tell in dialogue must be compressed into song |
| 9 | **Thematic Crystallisation** | The show's central idea needs to land with full force; only song can carry the thematic weight |
| 10 | **Reprise/Echo** | An earlier emotional moment recurs with new meaning; song unlocks the resonance |

Multiple triggers can apply — identify the primary one and any secondary.

### 3. Determine Song Voice
This must be decided before any lyric is written:

- **Internal / Soliloquy**: The character sings to themselves, to the audience, or to an absent/imagined person. The other character (if any) does not hear or respond to the song as song. Uses "I/he/she/they". Creates dramatic irony, interiority, longing. *(e.g., On My Own — Éponine sings about Marius; he is absent. Being Alive — Bobby addresses an imagined "someone". Somewhere Over the Rainbow — Dorothy to herself and the audience.)*
- **External / Direct**: The character sings directly to another character who is present, listening, and participating in the musical moment. Uses "you". Escalates the relationship — they are in the song together. *(e.g., All I Ask of You, Anything You Can Do, People Will Say We're in Love)*
- **Mixed**: Begins as one and shifts to the other. *(e.g., starts as internal revelation, the other character enters and it becomes direct address)*

### 4. Identify What Changes
A song with no change is a song that should not exist. State explicitly:
- **Emotional state at start of song**: what is the character feeling/thinking as the first note sounds?
- **Emotional state at end of song**: what has changed — in the character, the relationship, or the audience's understanding?
- **What the song does to the story**: does it escalate, reveal, commit, grieve, decide, or connect?

### 5. Check the Song Earns Its Place
Before writing the brief, confirm:
- [ ] Would cutting this song lose something irreplaceable from the story?
- [ ] Has the scene built sufficient emotional heat to justify the moment of song?
- [ ] Is this the right moment in the structural arc for this type of song? (Check Song Placement Map)
- [ ] Is this not a soliloquy song in disguise with no dramatic function beyond "character feels things"?

### 6. Create the Song Brief
The Song Brief is the handoff document to the lyricist. It becomes the basis of the Song Sheet.

---
**SONG BRIEF**

**Working Title**: [placeholder or draft title]
**Act / Scene**: [position]
**Character(s)**: [who sings]
**Song Type**: [opening_number / i_want_song / love_song / charm_song / comedy_number / duet / ensemble_number / narrative_exposition / act_one_finale / eleven_oclock_number / reprised_theme]
**Primary Song Trigger**: [from framework above]
**Song Voice**: Internal / External / Mixed
**Emotional State — Start**: [what the character is feeling as music begins]
**Emotional State — End**: [what has changed by the final note]
**What the Song Does to the Story**: [escalates / reveals / commits / grieves / decides / connects]
**Tone / Style**: [e.g., intimate ballad, uptempo comic number, anthemic ensemble, rock, wry character song]
**Reference Songs** (touchstone feel/approach): [1-3 examples from canon]
**The Last Spoken Line** (the final thing said before the song): [exact line from the scene]
**Key Image or Metaphor** (if any have emerged in the scene): [optional — a recurring image, object, or metaphor that could anchor the lyric]
**What the Character Cannot Say in Speech**: [the emotional truth the lyric must reach]
**Notes for Lyricist**: [any specific craft considerations — prosody challenges, rhythm, reprise connection, etc.]

---

## Output
- Song Trigger analysis with primary and secondary triggers identified
- Song Voice determination with reasoning
- Completed Song Brief document
- Recommendation on structural placement confirmation (does this align with the Song Placement Map?)
