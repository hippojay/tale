# Lyric Polish Workflow

## Objective
Rigorously review and polish an existing lyric draft for prosody, rhyme quality, character voice, structural integrity, and dramatic arc. This is the lyric equivalent of a line edit — not rewriting, but making every word and stress earn its place.

## Prerequisites
- Existing lyric draft (from Song Sheet or Write Lyric workflow)
- Song Sheet with trigger, voice, and emotional arc specifications
- Character profile for singing character(s)

## Steps

### 1. Read Aloud — Twice
Before any analysis, read the entire lyric aloud twice:
- First pass: read as speech, ignoring any imagined melody
- Second pass: mark any word or phrase where the reading felt awkward, rushed, or unnatural
These are the prosody problem zones.

### 2. Prosody Audit
Go line by line. For each line, mark the natural speech stress, then consider where musical stress would fall (downbeats, strong beats):
- [ ] Does the natural stress of speech align with likely musical stress?
- [ ] Are any important words on weak beats? (e.g., "I" on a downbeat when "love" should land there)
- [ ] Are any syllables being stretched unnaturally to fit a phrase? (flag these — they fight the singer)
- [ ] Is syntax natural? Would this character actually say it this way?

**Fix**: reorder, substitute, or rephrase — never sacrifice natural speech stress for a rhyme.

### 3. Rhyme Quality Audit
Check every end rhyme:
- [ ] Is it a perfect rhyme? (same vowel sound, same consonant ending)
- [ ] Is it a near-rhyme or sight rhyme (looks right on paper, sounds wrong aloud)? Near-rhymes land like "a wet dishcloth" — fix or abandon rhyme entirely for that couplet
- [ ] If rhyme is abandoned in a section: do the line endings use clearly different vowel sounds? (so the audience doesn't think you tried and failed)
- [ ] Does any rhyme feel forced — i.e., did the rhyme dictate the line rather than the meaning dictating the rhyme?

**Check internal rhyme**: if present, does it feel earned and musical, or clever-for-its-own-sake?

### 4. Character Voice Audit
- [ ] Could only this character sing these words? Or could any character?
- [ ] Is the vocabulary consistent with the character's established register? (no words they wouldn't use)
- [ ] Is the writer's hand visible — showing off vocabulary, imagery, or complexity beyond the character?
- [ ] Does the lyric sound like dialogue set to music, or like poetry?

### 5. Active Song Audit
- [ ] What is the character's emotional state on the first note?
- [ ] What is their emotional state on the final note?
- [ ] If these are the same: the song has no arc. It must be restructured.
- [ ] Does the Bridge (B section) earn the final A section — does the breakthrough in the Bridge change the meaning of the return?
- [ ] Is the final line the strongest line? It should be.

### 6. Specificity and Image Audit
- [ ] Does the lyric use specific, concrete imagery or generalised abstractions?
- [ ] Are there any "villagers on the green" lines — generic, non-character-specific sentiments that could belong to anyone?
- [ ] Is the central image or metaphor consistent through the song, or does it drift?
- [ ] Is anything being said explicitly that the image or music is already saying? Cut it.

### 7. Brevity Audit
- [ ] Is every line necessary?
- [ ] Can any line be said in fewer words or syllables without losing meaning?
- [ ] Are choruses developing with each repeat, or simply repeating?
- [ ] Is the Coda (if present) earning its additional length?

### 8. Reprise Potential Check (if applicable)
- If this song may be reprised: which line or phrase has the most resonance potential?
- Is that line positioned and crafted to carry new meaning in a different emotional context?

### 9. Compile Polish Notes
For each issue found:
- Line number and current text
- Problem type (prosody / rhyme / character voice / arc / specificity / brevity)
- Suggested revision or direction for revision
- Severity: Must Fix / Recommended / Consider

### 10. Deliver Polished Draft
Rewrite the lyric incorporating all Must Fix changes and Recommended changes. Preserve the writer's intent and character voice — polish is not replacement.

Present:
- Polished lyric (fully labelled with structural sections)
- Change log: what was changed and why
- Remaining open questions (if any lines need composer input on rhythm)

## Output
- Polished lyric draft with structural labels
- Polish notes with change log
- Any flags for the book writer (Alice) if the lyric now suggests changes to surrounding book scenes
