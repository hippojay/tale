# Musical Structure Workflow

## Objective
Design the complete two-act structure for a musical, placing every song in the dramatic arc and creating a beat sheet that shows how book and score work together.

## Prerequisites
- SRD or project concept (title, premise, central conflict, protagonist want vs. need)
- Musical style (book musical, concept musical, rock musical, etc.)
- Approximate target running time (standard: ~2.5 hours including intermission)

## Steps

### 1. Establish Central Dramatic Question
- What does the protagonist WANT? (external, concrete goal)
- What does the protagonist NEED? (internal, emotional truth — often opposite to the want)
- What is the central conflict preventing the want?
- What is the show's thematic argument? (the statement the finale must prove or disprove)

### 2. Two-Act Structure Mapping

**ACT ONE — Establish the world, the want, and the impossibility:**
- **Opening Number**: Establish world, tone, and rules. Introduces ensemble or protagonist. Sets genre contract with the audience. *(e.g., Comedy Tonight, Alexander Hamilton, Willkommen)*
- **Protagonist Introduction**: Who is this person before the story changes them?
- **I Want Song**: The protagonist's fundamental desire, stated early (within 15-20 minutes). Can be Internal (sung to self/audience) or External (sung to another character). *(e.g., Somewhere That's Green, Part of Your World, Being Good Isn't Good Enough)*
- **Inciting Incident**: The event that makes the want urgent and the conflict unavoidable
- **Rising Complications**: 2-3 book scenes and songs escalating stakes; introduce antagonist pressure, relationships, B-story
- **Act 1 Finale / Showstopper**: Escalates central conflict to a point of no return. Stakes double. World changes. Often ensemble. Never resolves — ends on unresolved desire, danger, or impossible choice. *(e.g., One Day More, Defying Gravity, The Confrontation)*

**ACT TWO — Test whether the want is the right thing to want:**
- **Act 2 Opener**: Re-engages audience, re-establishes stakes, often lighter in tone before the turn
- **Rising Complications Continue**: The protagonist pursues the want but at increasing cost
- **Dark Night / All Is Lost**: The want is apparently gone; the need becomes visible
- **11 O'Clock Number**: Penultimate song. The protagonist's deepest emotional truth, stripped of all defence. Often a solo. The character must confront what they truly are or what they truly need. *(e.g., Being Alive, Rose's Turn, I'm Still Here, Finishing the Hat)*
- **Climax / Resolution**: Final confrontation; want vs. need resolved
- **Finale / Reprise**: Thematic crystallisation; often a reprise that lands with new meaning

### 3. Song Placement Map
For each planned song, document:
- **Position** in act/scene
- **Song Type**: opening_number / i_want_song / love_song / charm_song / comedy_number / duet / ensemble_number / narrative_exposition / act_one_finale / eleven_oclock_number / reprised_theme
- **Song Trigger** (from the Song Trigger Framework):
  1. Declaration of Want — character must voice deepest desire or dream
  2. Emotional Overflow — feeling exceeds what speech can carry
  3. Conflict Escalation — argument speech can no longer contain
  4. Decision/Revelation — character at a turning point, processing internally
  5. Communal Expression — group must speak/feel/act as one
  6. Comic Exaggeration — situation so absurd only music can match it
  7. Seduction/Persuasion — character winning over another person or the audience
  8. Narrative Compression — story too long for dialogue
  9. Thematic Crystallisation — the show's central idea crystallised
  10. Reprise/Echo — earlier moment revisited with new meaning
- **Song Voice**: Internal/Soliloquy | External/Direct | Mixed
- **Character(s)**: who sings it
- **What changes**: what is different at the end of the song vs. the beginning

### 4. Character Arc Integration
- Map protagonist's want vs. need arc across all songs
- Identify which songs mark turning points in each major character arc
- Ensure the antagonist has sufficient dramatic weight (song or scene)
- Map the B-story (secondary relationship/theme) through the song list
- Check for tonal variety: comedy numbers, intimate moments, ensemble showstoppers

### 5. Pacing Assessment
- No two consecutive songs of the same type or emotional register
- Alternate between solo, duet, and ensemble for texture
- Act 2 should feel shorter in emotional time than Act 1 (accelerating)
- The 11 o'clock number must be preceded by a quieter or more comic moment to make it land

### 6. Musical Style Considerations
- **Book Musical**: Songs integrated with scenes; book scenes must earn each number
- **Concept Musical**: Songs may carry narrative directly; looser scene structure acceptable
- **Rock/Pop Musical**: Genre voice must be consistent; verse-chorus form common; energy is structural
- **Sung-Through**: All dramatic beats carried in song; recitative or arioso bridges scenes
- **Chamber Musical**: Intimacy is the strength; avoid anthemic overreach; psychological detail in lyric

## Output
- Complete beat sheet with act and scene positions
- Song Placement Map (table: position, song title placeholder, type, trigger, voice, character, what changes)
- Act structure summary with structural strengths and gaps identified
- Recommendations for any structural adjustments
