# Write Book Scene Workflow

## Objective
Write a single musical theatre book scene in industry-standard libretto format, advancing the story and building to the moment where the scene either tips into a song or hands off to the next scene.

## Prerequisites
- Scene position in the structure (Act X, Scene Y)
- Characters involved and their objectives in this scene
- What the scene must accomplish dramatically (what changes?)
- Whether the scene leads into a song — and if so, which Song Trigger applies

## Steps

### 1. Scene Setup
- Identify the scene's **dramatic function**: what must be different at the end vs. the beginning?
- Establish each character's **objective** in the scene (what do they want right now?)
- Identify the **obstacle**: what is preventing them getting it?
- Determine the **turn**: the moment the scene shifts — a revelation, a refusal, an escalation
- If scene leads to a song: identify the **Song Trigger** and the **Song Voice** (Internal/External/Mixed)

### 2. Format Rules — Industry Standard Libretto Format

Use the following conventions throughout:

```
ACT ONE
Scene 1

(Stage direction describing setting and opening action. Stage directions
are written in present tense, in italics or parentheses, describing only
what an audience can see or hear.)

CHARACTER NAME
Spoken dialogue here. Characters speak naturally — no verse,
no heightened language unless that is their established voice.

OTHER CHARACTER
Response here.

CHARACTER NAME
    (physical action or tone — used sparingly)
More dialogue.

                    "SONG TITLE"
            (CHARACTER begins to sing.)

CHARACTER NAME (sung)
    Lyric line here
    Second line here
```

**Key format rules:**
- ACT and SCENE headings: centered, all caps
- Stage directions: parenthetical or italic; present tense; describe only what is visible/audible
- Character names before dialogue: all caps, left-aligned
- Dialogue: regular prose, left-aligned under character name
- Parentheticals (tone/action): indented, in parentheses, used sparingly — only when meaning is ambiguous without them
- Song cue: centered, song title in quotes or caps, followed by stage direction indicating who begins singing
- Lyrics: indented, attributed to singing character(s)
- Never use camera directions (this is stage, not screen)

### 3. Write the Scene
- Open in the middle of something — avoid small talk, greetings, exposition dumps
- Each exchange must shift the power or information balance slightly
- Dialogue sounds like people talking, not people explaining — characters rarely say what they mean
- Physical action and staging should be implicit in the dialogue and directions
- If the scene leads to a song: build the emotional heat until speech becomes inadequate; the final line before the song should feel like the last thing that could be said

### 4. The Handoff to Song
If the scene ends in a musical number:
- The character's final spoken line should be the limit of what speech can express
- The transition to song should feel inevitable, not mechanical
- Note which Song Trigger applies — this governs the lyric brief
- Note the Song Voice — Internal (character sings to self/audience, other person may not hear) or External (sung directly to present character)

Common handoff patterns:
- **Declaration of Want**: character has been denied or frustrated to the point where they must declare the want aloud
- **Emotional Overflow**: emotion has been building; the final beat breaks the dam
- **Conflict Escalation**: argument reaches a point where words aren't enough — the next escalation is musical
- **Decision/Revelation**: character has just understood something that changes everything; must process it in song

### 5. Scene Economy Check
- Enter as late as possible; exit as early as possible
- Cut any exchange that doesn't advance objective, reveal character, or escalate conflict
- Parentheticals: no more than one per page unless absolutely necessary
- Stage directions: lean — describe what matters, trust directors and actors for the rest

### 6. Continuity Check
- Verify against Musical Bible / Story Bible for character voice consistency
- Check character knowledge: what does each character know at this point in the story?
- Ensure physical staging possibilities are consistent with the venue scale (if specified)

## Output
- Formatted book scene in industry-standard libretto format
- Scene notes: dramatic function, what changed, song handoff trigger and voice (if applicable)
