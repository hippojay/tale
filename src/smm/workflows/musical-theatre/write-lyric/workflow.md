# Write Lyric Workflow

## Objective
Write a complete, craft-quality musical theatre lyric from a Song Brief or Song Sheet, applying the principles of active songwriting, proper form, prosody, character voice, and emotional arc.

## Prerequisites
- Completed Song Brief or Song Sheet (with trigger, voice, emotional arc, and dramatic function)
- Character profile for singing character(s)
- Musical style of the show (affects song form and register)

## Steps

### 1. Read and Absorb the Brief
Before writing a word:
- Confirm **Song Voice**: Internal (I/he/she) or External/Direct (you) — this determines every pronoun
- Confirm **what changes**: if you cannot state clearly what is different at the end vs. the beginning, do not start writing
- Confirm **character voice**: what is distinctive about how this character speaks? Vocabulary level, rhythm, directness, humour, formality?
- Confirm **song form**: Verse-A-A-B-A or verse-chorus or through-composed
- Identify the **key image or metaphor** if one exists — this is the spine of the lyric

### 2. Identify the Emotional Journey
Map the song's internal arc before writing:
- **Opening emotional state**: where is the character as the first note sounds?
- **The build**: how does the feeling escalate, complicate, or deepen through A1 and A2?
- **The Bridge (B section)**: this is the breakthrough — the moment the character discovers or admits something they couldn't face at the start. The Bridge must earn the final A section.
- **The final A section**: lands differently because of everything that came before it. Often the same words carry new meaning.
- **The coda** (if applicable): the landing — the character's new position

### 3. Find the Central Image
Great musical theatre lyrics work through a central metaphor or image rather than stating emotion directly. Come in through the back door:
- What object, action, or image in this character's world could carry the emotional weight?
- What would this character notice that another character wouldn't? (specificity)
- *(e.g., not "I love him" — but "My coloring book / with pretty pictures / where the colors never run" — Kander & Ebb)*
- *(e.g., not "I'm isolated" — but "Waving through a window" — Pasek & Paul)*

### 4. Write the Verse (if applicable)
The Verse sets the scene and context — it is more conversational, often less rhyme-bound:
- Establish the who, where, and emotional starting point
- Verse often uses a different rhyme scheme or no rhyme, loosening into the A section
- Keep it brief — the audience is waiting for the main section

### 5. Write A1 and A2
The core melodic sections that carry the main lyric:
- A1 states the proposition or feeling
- A2 develops, complicates, or escalates it — do not simply repeat A1 content
- Rhyme scheme should be consistent between A1 and A2 (they share the same melody)
- **Prosody check on every line**: read aloud and mark the natural speech stress. Does it align with where musical stress would fall? If not, rewrite.
- **Rhyme quality**: every end rhyme must be a true rhyme. If you cannot find one, use a different vowel sound entirely rather than a near-rhyme.

### 6. Write the Bridge (B Section / Release)
The Bridge is where the song earns its place:
- Different melodic territory — emotionally, the character breaks through to something new
- Often a shift in perspective, an admission, a decision, or a contradiction of what was sung in A
- Different rhyme scheme from A sections
- This is where the song pivots from feeling to understanding — or from wanting to deciding

### 7. Write the Final A Section
Return to the A melody, but the words must land differently:
- Same or similar lyric to A1/A2 — but after the Bridge, it means something else
- Or a variation that shows what has changed
- The final line is the most important line in the song — it is what the audience will carry out of the theatre

### 8. Write the Coda (if applicable)
- Only if the song needs a landing beyond the final A
- Often shorter, fragmented, or a single crystallising line
- Never use the coda to explain what the song just said

### 9. Craft Checks

**Prosody audit** — read every line aloud:
- [ ] Every stressed syllable falls on a musically strong beat
- [ ] No contorted syntax forcing a rhyme ("And then into the room I came" = wrong)
- [ ] Natural speech rhythm preserved

**Rhyme audit**:
- [ ] Every intended rhyme is a perfect rhyme
- [ ] Any section without rhyme uses clearly different vowel sounds (so audience doesn't think you tried and failed)
- [ ] Internal rhyme (where present) feels earned, not forced

**Character voice**:
- [ ] Could only this character sing these words?
- [ ] Vocabulary is consistent with the character's established voice
- [ ] The writer's hand is not showing

**Active song check**:
- [ ] Something changes between the first note and the last
- [ ] The lyric does not end in the same emotional place it began
- [ ] The song could not be cut without losing something irreplaceable

**Brevity**:
- [ ] Every word earns its place
- [ ] No line states what the music or a previous line already says
- [ ] No chorus that repeats without developing

### 10. Label and Format the Final Draft

```
[SONG TITLE]
Character: [name(s)]
Song Voice: Internal / External / Mixed
Form: Verse-A-A-B-A / Verse-Chorus / Through-Composed

VERSE
[lyric]

A1
[lyric]

A2
[lyric]

BRIDGE
[lyric]

A3
[lyric]

CODA (if applicable)
[lyric]
```

## Output
- Complete labelled lyric draft
- Prosody and rhyme notes (any sections flagged for composer attention)
- Craft notes: character voice assessment, central image, what changes
- Saved to or incorporated into the Song Sheet for this number
